[[The populist backlash stems from economic disparity and powerlessness|Democracy is in danger]], and populist anger is its symptom. [[Mainstream Parties' Diagnosis of the Backlash|Mainstream parties read the backlash]] as a reaction to diversity and to workers' failure to adapt to [[Globalization]] — partly true, but a diagnosis that conveniently leaves the parties themselves out of the picture.

What the diagnosis hides is the parties' own forty-year [[Meritocratic Project]], propped up by [[Technocracy]]: a faith in [[market]] efficiency that brackets [[Moral|moral]] argument out of [[politics]] and reserves judgement for [[Credentials|credentialed]] experts. The trail starts with [[Reagan and Thatcher set the unchallenged market consensus|Reagan and Thatcher]] and runs through a [[Market-Friendly Liberalism|center-left]] that softened the consensus without breaking it[^1]. Market-driven [[Globalization|globalization]], in turn, [[Globalization weakened mutual dependence on local economies|loosened the ties]] that held local [[Tribe|groups]] together.

The bill comes due in two ways. The contest no longer pays out — [[The American Dream does not payout|the American Dream bargain is broken]], and [[The richest 1% get more money than the bottom half combined|the gap between top and bottom is staggering]] — so [[From Inequality to Loss of Esteem|grievances about material inequality migrate into grievances about esteem]]. And the [[Populist revolts hit liberal parties hardest|populist revolts that follow]] aim at the elite but hit the liberal parties hardest.

This is why the political divide no longer maps cleanly onto right vs left, but onto [[Open vs Closed|globalists vs tribalists]].
